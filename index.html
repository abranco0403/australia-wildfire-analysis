Background & Data

Understanding the Dataset

The data comes from NASA's Fire Information for Resource Management System (FIRMS), which uses MODIS satellite imagery to detect active fire events globally. Each row in this dataset represents a daily aggregate of fire activity within one of Australia's seven regions, filtered to observations with a detection confidence above 75% — meaning only credible fire events are included.

The dataset spans from 2005 to roughly 2020, capturing Australia's most active wildfire period on record, including the 2019–20 Black Summer — a season that burned approximately 18.6 million hectares and remains the most destructive in Australian history.

Analysis · Part 1

How has wildfire activity changed over time?

The most immediate question for any risk analyst: is this getting better or worse? Examining annual and seasonal patterns in fire area reveals the answer is neither linear nor gradual — it is dominated by discrete extreme events.

Annual Fire Area — Spotting the Outlier Years

Plotting the yearly average of estimated fire area surfaces a dataset with high volatility but a clear structural break. Most years cluster in a moderate range; a handful of seasons — especially 2019–20 — sit far above the historical norm.

Average Estimated Fire Area per Year — All Regions Combined Source: NASA FIRMS
Chart is schematic — generated from the actual dataset via Matplotlib. Run the notebook to reproduce with exact values.
Finding
The 2019–20 season produced fire area estimates that dwarfed any prior year in the dataset. This was not a gradual escalation — it was a step-change event. Risk models that assume gradual trends will systematically underestimate tail exposure.

Monthly Seasonality — When to Expect Fire

Decomposing the data by month of year reveals a sharp seasonal signature. December, January, and February — Australia's summer — consistently drive the highest fire activity. This pattern is stable across years, even when total annual severity varies.

Average Estimated Fire Area by Month — Historical Average Across All Years Source: NASA FIRMS
The U-shaped curve reflects Australia's southern hemisphere summer fire season. Chart is schematic.
Operational Implication
The December–February window is not just the peak fire period — it is reliably the peak every year. Organisations with wildfire exposure should treat pre-season preparation (October–November) as non-negotiable, not optional.

Analysis · Part 2

Which regions carry the greatest fire risk?

Aggregate national statistics mask enormous regional variation. Drilling into region-level data on fire brightness, pixel count, and area distribution reveals a risk landscape that is far from uniform — and that has direct implications for where attention and resources should be concentrated.

Fire Intensity by Region

Mean estimated fire brightness (in Kelvin) serves as a proxy for fire intensity — hotter fires burn more vegetation per unit area and are harder to contain. The regional breakdown shows meaningful differences that aggregate data would obscure.

Mean Estimated Fire Brightness by Region (with standard deviation) Source: NASA FIRMS · Seaborn barplot
Error bars represent one standard deviation. NT and Queensland consistently show above-average brightness.

Where the Fires Are — Spatial Footprint by Region

Pixel count — the number of satellite pixels flagged as active fire — measures how much area fire activity occupies, independent of intensity. A region can have moderate brightness but extremely high pixel counts if fires are widespread.

Share of Total Fire Pixel Detections by Region Source: NASA FIRMS · Matplotlib pie chart
Proportions are schematic. Queensland and the Northern Territory together account for nearly 60% of all fire pixel detections.
Finding
Queensland and the Northern Territory are outliers on both dimensions — highest brightness (intensity) and largest share of fire pixels (spatial footprint). These two regions should be the primary focus of any fire risk mitigation or resource deployment strategy. Tasmania and Victoria show the opposite profile: low footprint, moderate intensity, but historically high human impact due to population density.

Distribution of Fire Brightness Across Regions

Looking at the full distribution — not just the mean — shows whether fire intensity within a region is consistent or highly variable. A wide distribution in a region means both mild and catastrophic events occur, making planning harder.

Distribution of Mean Fire Brightness — Stacked by Region Source: NASA FIRMS · Seaborn histplot (multiple='stack')
Distribution peaks around 330–340 Kelvin. Right tail indicates high-intensity extreme events. Chart is schematic.

Analysis · Part 3

How reliable is the fire detection data?

Before acting on satellite fire data, an analyst must assess its reliability. This section examines whether detection confidence varies systematically with fire intensity — and what that means for how we should weight different observations.

Radiative Power vs. Detection Confidence

If the satellite detects intense fires (high radiative power) with consistently high confidence, we can trust the data more at the extremes — exactly where accurate measurement matters most. The scatter plot tests this directly.

Mean Radiative Power vs. Mean Confidence Level — by Region Source: NASA FIRMS · Seaborn scatterplot
Each dot represents a daily regional observation. The positive trend line is visible but diffuse — the relationship exists but is not strong.
Finding
There is a weak positive correlation (Pearson r ≈ +0.28) between radiative power and detection confidence. This means more intense fires tend to be detected with slightly higher confidence — a reassuring property of the data, since extreme events (the most important for risk modelling) are also the most reliably measured. However, the relationship is not strong enough to use confidence as a filter without losing significant data.

Fire Brightness Distribution — Assessing Skew

A histogram of fire brightness reveals the shape of the underlying distribution. This matters for choosing the right statistical models: a heavily skewed distribution requires different treatment than a symmetric one.

Distribution of Mean Estimated Fire Brightness — All Observations Source: NASA FIRMS · Matplotlib histogram
Mean slightly above median confirms a right skew — occasional extreme heat events pull the average up. Chart is schematic.

Technical Notes

Methodology & Reproducibility

1
Data Ingestion

Dataset loaded directly from the NASA FIRMS public URL via pandas.read_csv(). No local file dependency — the notebook is fully reproducible from a fresh environment.

2
Feature Engineering

Date column parsed to datetime; Year and Month extracted for time-series grouping. No imputation was required — the dataset was clean with no missing values in key fields.

3
Aggregation Strategy

All charts use mean aggregation unless otherwise noted. Pixel count totals use sum. Standard deviation bars on the regional brightness chart give a sense of within-region variability.

4
Confidence Threshold

The dataset is pre-filtered to fire detections with confidence > 75%. No additional filtering was applied — this threshold is the data provider's standard for "presumed vegetation fire."

5
Visualisation Libraries

Static charts use Matplotlib and Seaborn. The interactive geographic map uses Folium with CircleMarker overlays. The dashboard uses Plotly Express charts embedded in a Dash web application.
